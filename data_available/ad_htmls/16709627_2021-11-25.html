You will find a large courtyard to the rear of the property behind original cut stone walls with one of the heated gazebos, fantastic for entertaining, the second gazebo overlooks the waterfront where you will access a private fresh water beach at the waters edge along with a large plot of ground towards the gated entrance.\r\n\r\nHistorical Note\r\n\r\n\r\nBallywilliam House, Kinsale set behind wrought iron gates and a mere 6kms from Kinsale Town Centre is not only a spectacular property but one that is steeped in local & national history from as early as 1765. \r\n\r\nBallywilliam House is believed to be the seat of the Barter family and the house has existed in some form on lands at Ballywilliam since 1765, when the Barter family first took up residence in County Cork. William Barter is believed to have built the house that still stands today in 1816. Several members of the Barter family held lands in and around Kinsale in the 1870's, including Benjamin Barter, who owner over 400 acres at Ballywilliam as wall as lands in another local area, Ballinhassig. \r\n\r\n\r\nOn Saturday 27th April 1839, Benjamin Barter offered Ballywilliam House, out-offices and demesne of 100 acres for lease. He highlighted the demesne's salmon fishery and quay as one of the main features. \r\n\r\nBy 1870, Benjamin was back living in Ballywilliam House accompanied by his wife, Mary. Benjamin later died in 1874, in 1875 Mary advertised the property for lease and less then 10 years later she offered the property for sale along with other lands held by the family near Ballygarvan. The property was purchased in 1886 privately by a solicitor. It was eventually revealed that the purchaser was a local dispensary doctor known as Dr. George Vickery. \r\n\r\nDr. Vickery continued his work as a dispensary doctor in the town of Kinsale until he retired in 1920. By this time, the War of Independence was underway, leading ultimately to the occupation of Kinsale town by Anti-Treaty forces in July 1922. \r\n\r\nAs the Free State forces advanced on the town, the Anti-Treaty IRA began to occupy strategic positions around the town to prepare for its defence. As Ballywilliam House overlooked Bandon River and across, which the Free State army eventually attacked, Ballywilliam House was the perfect vantage point. The Anti Treaty troops arrived at the house late one night and informed Dr. Vickery that they needed to occupy the property. Dr. Vickery testily responded that he knew every one of them and had brought many of them into the world, and that they could go away and stopped bothering him. The Anti Treaty army, mostly young men, complied and left the property. \r\n\r\nThe Vickery family resided at Ballywilliam until 1960. It then became the residence of the Webb family until 1970, when it was later bought by the Bray Family. \r\n\r\nThe prior owners to the current vendor, purchased the property in 1995 and lovingly restored the house both inside & out. They wanted to ensure the property stayed true to its original form and spirit which they did so beautifully. \r\n\r\nIt was later revealed that the keen purchaser of the property in 1995 had a familiar name and voice to the locals of Kinsale, Tori Amos. It is widely known that Ballywilliam House was home to many of her works such as her album, The Boys of Pele as well as many more. Amos transformed the property into a residential recording studio whilst working on the above mentioned album in 1995 and later in 2011 the classical label Deutsche Grammophon released Night Hunters, Tori's 12th album, the photographs for this project were also taken on the premises along with the video for Star Whisperer, which was designed for a song on the album. Ballywilliam House has been a place of creativity for both Tori and a multitude of other musicians who have recorded at the house also. Tori has been quoted referring to the property as 'magical' and home to 'many memories, both personal & artistic.'\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\nEntrance Hall-11.59m x 4.57m\r\nDouble glass panel doors through to staircase hall.
